# The Frontier Manifesto

*for Forward Deployed Engineering*

We are engineers who build inside our customers' walls: shipping against real data, in real environments, for outcomes the customer would call their own. Practicing this, we have come to value:

**Outcomes** over deliverables
**Production** over demos
**Firsthand context** over written requirements
**The product** over the project
**Exits** over residencies

The items on the right have value. When they conflict, the items on the left decide.

## Principles

1. **Go where the context is.** Enterprise software fails for missing context, not missing code. Context lives at the customer and degrades in transmission. Move the engineer, not the documents.

2. **Start in the data, not the requirements.** What people say they need and what their data shows they need are different documents. Read the second one first.

3. **Deploy the platform; build the last mile.** The engagement starts from the product's existing capability, not from an empty repo. Field code is the last mile between the platform and the outcome — and last-mile code written twice is a platform gap.

4. **Ship something real inside two weeks.** The first working software on real data is the engagement's ignition. Most of it should be assembly, not construction; with the platform underneath and an agent-assisted toolchain, two weeks is generous — when it slips, the blocker is access, not engineering.

5. **Show, discuss, ship again.** The heartbeat is a short loop: working software in front of the people who own the outcome, feedback taken, next version shipped. Days, not quarters. For probabilistic systems, "working" is an eval score, not a demo that went well.

6. **Overfit at the edge, generalize at the core.** Solving one customer exactly is correct — when the overfit is logged and temporary. Silent overfit is debt fraud.

7. **Every engagement feeds the product.** An engagement that leaves no trace in the product was consulting, whatever the job title said. Each extraction widens the platform, and every widening shortens the next engagement.

8. **Own the outcome, not the ticket.** Your accountability is a business result the customer recognizes as theirs, not a list of things delivered.

9. **Set the exit date on day one.** An engagement without an exit date is a services contract discovering itself.

10. **Adoption is half the job — staff it.** Someone must own workflow change, politics, and problem selection. Teams that drop this role pay for it in shelfware.

11. **Nobody lives at the frontier.** Rotation between field and core is structural. It carries context into the product and keeps field engineers product engineers.
