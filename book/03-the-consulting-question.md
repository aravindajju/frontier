# 3. The Consulting Question

There is a thread on Blind, the anonymous tech forum, whose title has become the role's unofficial hazing ritual: ["wtf is a forward deployed engineer bro youre just a consultant."](https://www.teamblind.com/post/wtf-is-a-forward-deployed-engineer-bro-youre-just-a-consultant-lurvwz6p) Every practitioner has met some version of it — at a dinner, in a hiring debrief, from a skeptical CFO. The polished responses tend to make things worse: the more carefully you explain that you embed with customers, learn their domain, build them systems, and bill— well. The sentence doesn't end anywhere comfortable.

This chapter's position is that the jab deserves a better answer than a flinch, and the better answer starts with a concession: sometimes it's true. There are teams wearing the FDE title today whose work is consulting in every respect that matters — bespoke builds, billable renewals, learning that accrues to the individual and walks out the door. The title doesn't settle the question. Nothing about standing in a customer's building makes you not a consultant, any more than standing in a garage makes you a car.

What makes the question worth a chapter is that it is decidable. Whether a given team is doing forward deployed engineering or consulting-with-equity is not a matter of framing or intent. It is a matter of structure, the structure shows up in artifacts and numbers, and anyone — an engineer weighing an offer, an executive reviewing a function, a customer wondering what they bought — can run the audit.

## The three structural differences

The first difference is what the engineer is accountable for. A consulting engagement is accountable for effort and deliverables: the statement of work enumerates what will be produced, and producing it is success, whether or not anything improves for the client. Forward deployed engineering is accountable for an outcome — a number the customer's executives already track, named in writing before work begins. The difference sounds rhetorical until you watch it operate. A team that owes a deliverable protects scope; a team that owes an outcome renegotiates scope constantly and protects the number. The Charter — this method's first artifact — exists to make the commitment inspectable: if there is no written outcome with a date on it, the accountability is deliverables, whatever anyone says in the kickoff.

The second difference is where the work starts. A consultant starts from an empty repository and the client's requirements; whatever gets built is the client's bespoke asset. A forward deployed engineer arrives carrying a platform and builds only the last mile between that platform's existing capability and this customer's outcome. This was the role's founding condition — Palantir's field engineers existed to land Gotham, not to build one-off systems — and it has a testable signature: look at the engagement's code. If it substantially duplicates what the vendor's product already does, the platform is a prop and the work is bespoke. This book names that failure the Greenfield Reflex, and its economics are unforgiving, because bespoke work scales linearly with customers while platform work compounds.

The third difference is what happens to the learning, and it is the decisive one. Consulting's business model is that expertise accumulates in people, who are then billed to the next client — the firm's asset is the bench. Forward deployed engineering's model is that expertise accumulates in the product: the repeated last mile becomes a platform capability, the platform widens, and the next customer needs less field work than this one did. The field function, run correctly, is engaged in shrinking its own necessity — which no consultancy has ever had an incentive to do. This too is auditable. The method's fourth metric, Extraction Rate, counts Ledger items generalized into the product per quarter. A field team whose extraction rate has been zero for three quarters is a consultancy, whatever its org chart says.

## History's referee

The strongest version of the skeptic's case doesn't come from Blind; it comes from enterprise software history, and [Thomas Otter states it best](https://thomasotter.substack.com/p/on-the-forward-deployed-engineer). In the 1970s, SAP's engineers worked directly on customer mainframes — forward deployed in everything but name, since the R/1-era company had no other way to ship. But SAP did not win the ERP market by scaling its field engineers. It won by relentlessly generalizing what those engineers learned into standard product, and by handing implementation to an ecosystem of partners. The field era was a phase the company grew through, not a model it kept. Otter's warning lands on the sharpest edge of the current fashion: "if the FDE is billable, they are working for the project, not the product." Billability is a loyalty test, and it is failed quietly, one renewal at a time. Marty Cagan's framing, which Otter invokes, draws the same boundary from the product side: field engineers build prototypes against real customers; the product organization's job is to be constantly generalizing — the field function is tolerable exactly as long as the generalization machine behind it runs.

The counter-referee is Palantir's own ledger, told in chapter 2: a company that looked like a consultancy in 2016 and posted roughly 80 percent gross margins in 2023, because the extraction machine — Foundry — worked. Both histories agree on the mechanism and differ only in emphasis: the field phase creates the knowledge; the productization loop decides whether the knowledge becomes an asset or a payroll. [Rocio Wu's analysis](https://www.forbes.com/sites/rociowu/2026/02/05/the-uncomfortable-truth-about-fdes/) gives the operational corollary this book adopts: field engineering should function as temporary scaffolding, never as permanent architecture — and when more than a third of deployments need heavy field effort for the same class of problem, the honest reading is a product defect, not a staffing shortage. That threshold appears in this method as the one-third rule, and it makes the scaffolding claim falsifiable quarter by quarter.

## The title is diluting while you read this

There is a second, newer reason to take definitions seriously. Martin Fowler coined the term [semantic diffusion](https://martinfowler.com/bliki/SemanticDiffusion.html) for what happens when a useful term spreads faster than its meaning: each borrower keeps the word and sheds a constraint, until the word means roughly nothing. It happened to *agile*; it happened to *DevOps* — a philosophy that ended up as a job title for build engineers, to its founders' documented dismay. It is happening to *forward deployed engineer* now, at the speed of a hiring boom. [The Pragmatic Engineer's survey of 2026 postings](https://blog.pragmaticengineer.com/the-pulse-forward-deployed-engineering-heats-up-again/) found FDE roles that were a quarter coding and half meetings — indistinguishable from solutions architecture — and labs standing up FDE consulting arms as separate entities, without the parent company's equity: an org-chart confession that the work had drifted from the product.

Dilution is not a branding problem; it is a market-for-lemons problem. When the title stops carrying information, customers can't tell the productizing teams from the body shops, engineers can't tell which jobs build the founder skill stack and which build PowerPoint, and the diluted majority prices the disciplined minority's reputation. The defense is the unfashionable one: a definition with edges. Production code in the customer's environment against real data; ownership of a named outcome; an obligation to leave a trace in the product. The bright-line test is deliberately strict enough to exclude most of what currently wears the title — that is what it is for. A test that everything passes protects no one.

## Living with the question

The consulting question, properly handled, is not an insult to rebut but a standing audit to pass — quarterly, in numbers, for as long as the function exists. The gravitational pull toward consulting is real and permanent: renewals flatter revenue, customers will happily rent a good engineer forever, and every incentive in an account team points toward the Permanent Resident. A field function stays on the right side of the line only by structure — charters with exit dates, ledgers with dispositions, an extraction rate someone is accountable for — because nothing else survives contact with a good quarter.

Which raises the underlying question this part of the book still owes an answer to: why being there works at all — why the expensive, awkward, dilution-prone act of putting an engineer inside a customer's building beats every cheaper alternative for a certain class of software. That argument is next.
