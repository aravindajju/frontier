# 2. Where This Came From

In the mid-2000s, Palantir had a problem that no amount of conventional product development could solve. Its customers were intelligence and defense agencies — the CIA among the earliest — and those customers could not behave the way software customers are supposed to. They could not share their data with a vendor; much of it was classified. They could not write requirements documents; the analysts who understood the work were not allowed to describe it in transmittable detail, and often could not articulate it anyway. The standard enterprise software playbook — gather requirements, build at headquarters, ship an installer — failed at its first step, [as accounts of the company's early years describe](https://fde.academy/blog/how-palantir-invented-the-forward-deployed-engineer-model).

Palantir's answer was to stop trying to move the information and move the engineers instead. Engineers went inside the customer's buildings, sat with the analysts, watched the actual work, and built against the actual data — because there was no other way to learn what to build. The name they eventually gave the role borrowed military vocabulary the customers already spoke: a forward-deployed unit is one stationed in the operational theater rather than at home base. The earliest published reference to the title dates to 2010, and the role was formalized inside Palantir in the early 2010s, [per the history assembled on Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forward_Deployed_Engineer) and [The Pragmatic Engineer's reporting](https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/p/forward-deployed-engineers).

It is worth being precise about the purpose, because it is the part most modern imitators miss. The forward deployed engineer was never conceived as a bespoke-systems builder. Palantir had a platform — Gotham — and the FDE existed to get that platform implemented, adopted, and producing results inside customers who could not implement it themselves. The field role was platform-first from its founding: deploy what exists, build what's missing, and carry what's missing back home. That loop, not the embedding, turned out to be the invention.

## Delta and Echo

Internally, Palantir named its engineering split with radio-alphabet shorthand, [documented in the company's own blog](https://blog.palantir.com/dev-versus-delta-demystifying-engineering-roles-at-palantir-ad44c2a6e87). A **Dev** built the product: one capability, many customers. A **Delta** — the forward deployed engineer — inverted it: one customer, many capabilities. Deltas passed the same technical bar as Devs; the company described the profile as something like a scrappy startup CTO, at home writing production code inside broken data environments with unreasonable deadlines. Tellingly, Deltas reported into business development, not engineering — the field role lived next to revenue from the start.

The second half of the structure is the part history has been unkind to, because almost everyone copying Palantir drops it. Alongside the Delta worked the **Echo** — the Deployment Strategist, [profiled in another Palantir post](https://blog.palantir.com/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-palantir-deployment-strategist-951cb59a5a96). Echoes were usually not software engineers. They owned what the software could not: which problem to solve first, whose workflow would change, which institutional antibodies would attack the deployment, and how to win the meeting where the department head decides whether to cooperate. The pairing encoded a claim this book treats as a principle: adoption is half the job, and it deserves its own accountable owner.

The scale of the commitment is easy to underestimate. Until around 2016, [Palantir had more forward deployed engineers than product engineers](https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/p/forward-deployed-engineers). Field engineers were onsite three and four days a week; the culture said get on a plane first and ask questions later. The company took the human side seriously enough to train FDEs on status dynamics — handing out copies of Keith Johnstone's *Impro*, a book about improvisational theater, because reading a room is a learnable skill — a detail from [Nabeel Qureshi's "Reflections on Palantir"](https://nabeelqu.substack.com/p/reflections-on-palantir), the most-cited first-hand account of the role and a source this book returns to repeatedly.

## The extraction that built a product

Through the mid-2010s, the obvious critique wrote itself: this is a consultancy with stock options. In 2016, Qureshi notes, the company's economics looked the part. What changed the answer was not a rebranding but an extraction. Field teams kept rebuilding the same things — data ingestion pipelines, visualization layers, operational UIs — one customer at a time. The company generalized them: ingestion became a product called Magritte, visualization became Contour, interface-building became Workshop, and the assembled result became **Foundry**, the platform that came to drive the majority of Palantir's commercial revenue. By 2023 the company's gross margins were around 80 percent — software margins, not the roughly 32 percent a services firm like Accenture runs — [a contrast Qureshi draws directly](https://nabeelqu.substack.com/p/reflections-on-palantir).

That sequence — field teams overfit, the core generalizes the residue, the platform widens, the next deployment gets shorter — is the productization loop, and it is the load-bearing fact of this whole history. It is the difference between the model being a margin problem and the model being a moat. It is also, not incidentally, why this book's method is built around the Ledger and the Extraction Review rather than around embedding for its own sake: the embedding is the easy part to copy, and the loop is the part that pays.

One more measurable trace of those years: the alumni. Ex-Palantir FDEs went on to found companies at a rate far out of proportion to the company's size — Qureshi counts more ex-Palantir founders than ex-Google founders in Y Combinator batches, from a company one-fiftieth Google's headcount. Whatever else the role does, it manufactures the exact skill stack — technical depth, customer contact, outcome ownership, comfort with chaos — that founding demands. Chapter 17 returns to this, because it is the honest answer to how a field function retains ambitious engineers: you don't, forever, and the smart version of the model prices that in.

## Everyone's problem now

For fifteen years the model stayed mostly where it started — defense, intelligence, a handful of data-heavy enterprises — treated by the wider industry as a Palantir eccentricity. Then AI systems started shipping into enterprises, and the industry rediscovered Palantir's founding conditions at scale.

The resemblance is structural. A large language model, like a classified intelligence workflow, cannot be specified from a distance: the customer cannot write down what the system needs to do, because nobody knows until it runs against the real data, inside the real workflow, in front of the real users. Probabilistic systems demo in an afternoon and then fail in production in ways specific to each customer's data and processes. The gap between the demo and the deployed system became the industry's binding constraint — and the fix the industry converged on was Palantir's: put engineers in the field.

The adoption wave is documented and steep. [OpenAI stood up a forward deployed engineering team](https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/p/forward-deployed-engineers) under Colin Jarvis, whose definition of the role echoes the FDE test — FDEs write code directly on customer infrastructure with customer tooling, which is precisely what a solutions architect does not do. [Anthropic runs the function as Applied AI](https://getperspective.ai/blog/anthropic-applied-ai-engineers-forward-deployed-claude-enterprise), embedding engineers with strategic customers for multi-week sprints of evals, prompts, and production agents. [Google Cloud began hiring FDEs into its AI go-to-market organization](https://thenewstack.io/forward-deployed-engineer-fde-openai-google/); the AI application companies — Sierra, Harvey, Decagon, and most of their cohort — [hired FDEs as a default](https://www.paraform.com/blog/forward-deployed-ai-engineer); companies as far from defense as [Ramp built FDE pods](https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/p/forward-deployed-engineers). Job postings for the title grew by several hundred percent year over year — [north of 700 percent by some counts](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2026/forward-deployed-engineers-emerge-as-one-of-ais-fastest-growing-jobs/) — making it one of the fastest-growing roles in software.

One thing did change between Palantir's founding conditions and today's, and it changes the method's arithmetic rather than its logic. Palantir's field model needed armies — hence more Deltas than Devs. Today's forward deployed engineer builds with AI agents, and the systems being deployed are themselves AI systems. One or two engineers now produce what a deployed team once did, which is why this book's engagements are scoped in weeks, not quarters, and staffed in ones and twos, not squads. The history explains the shape of the job; the leverage explains the shape of the time-box.

It also explains the risk. A model this fashionable gets copied badly: the embedding without the loop, the title without the test, the margin costs without the extraction that redeems them. The rest of Part I is about telling the difference — starting with the accusation every practitioner has heard by now, which deserves a better answer than a flinch.
