# A Note on the Stories

The engineers in this book — Advik, Adit, and the people around them — are fictional. So are Meridian National Bank, Cascade Health, Granite Mutual, and every sponsor, analyst, and adjuster who appears alongside them. No real company or person is depicted, and any resemblance is the ordinary kind that comes from writing about work many people do.

The stories are composites, and they are built from real material: engagements the author has run, first-hand accounts published by practitioners of this work, and the documented history of the companies that invented it. The fax channel that generates most of the rework, the spreadsheet that is secretly the system of record, the summaries that invert liability on subrogation files, the pilot in its ninth month of demos — each is the kind of thing that happens, at the scale it happens, told with the specificity the real versions have and the names they don't.

Where a story carries a number — a rework rate, an eval result, a week count — the number is chosen to be typical, not reported. Where the book cites a real company by name, Palantir most often, it relies on the published record, and the sources are in Appendix E. The line between the two is kept deliberately bright: named companies are history; named engineers are craft, wearing fiction so the craft can be shown whole.
