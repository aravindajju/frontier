# 21. Scaling, and the Function's Own Graduation

The most consequential things Palantir's forward deployed engineers built were the ones that ended their own jobs. Engagement pattern after engagement pattern — ingestion, visualization, interface work — stopped being fought by hand and became the tools that stacked into Foundry (chapter 2 names them), which by the 2020s carried the majority of the company's revenue with a fraction of the field labor per account. Read as an organizational story rather than a product story, the arc is striking: the field function's finest output was the shrinking of its own necessity.

That is the end state this chapter argues for. Scaling a field function is not growing it until it is large. It is running it hot while systematically transferring its work into the platform — and being able to tell, on purpose, which stage of that transfer you are in.

## Scaling the work: portfolio and playbooks

One engagement is an adventure. Five concurrent engagements are a portfolio, and a portfolio changes what management means.

The unit of oversight shifts from the engagement to the pattern. The quarterly portfolio review — already in the method as the Extraction Review's wide-angle form — becomes the function's real steering wheel: Ledgers from every engagement on one table, read for repetition. What appears once is an anecdote. What appears three times is a roadmap item with evidence attached, and the one-third rule turns the reading into a referendum: when a third of the portfolio is fighting the same class of problem, the function has measured a product gap, and the honest response is core engineering capacity, not field headcount.

Repetition also yields playbooks — and they deserve precision, because they are the scaling instrument most functions get wrong. A playbook is a Landing-accelerator: the known shape of a claims-data engagement, the reference agent boundary for a bank, the eval-suite skeleton for a document-processing outcome, the Charter template pre-filled with the vertical's usual graduation criteria. Week one compresses toward day two. What a playbook must never become is a deployment script that replaces the Data Walk — the moment "we have a playbook for insurers" means "we stopped looking at this insurer's data," the function has begun manufacturing overfit at scale, uniformly, which is worse than doing it one engagement at a time because nothing flags it.

Ramp's field pods and the AI labs' regional field teams show the era's organizational forms: small units, tight vertical focus, platform-first mandates. The forms differ; the invariant is the portfolio review. A field function that scales engagements without scaling pattern-reading has built a fleet without navigation.

## The demand paradox, and the scaffolding answer

Scaling decisions happen under a paradox: success generates demand for the thing you are trying to need less of. Every graduated engagement produces references; references produce pipeline; pipeline produces pressure to grow the field team. Meanwhile extraction — the mechanism that would let the *product* absorb that pipeline — competes for the same engineers' time and never has a customer shouting for it. Left to demand signals alone, every field function grows toward consultancy, smoothly, while its Extraction Rate quietly falls.

Rocio Wu's formulation is the discipline: field engineering as temporary scaffolding, never permanent architecture. Scaffolding is not a slur — nothing gets built without it — but it is load-bearing only during construction, and the building that keeps its scaffolding up forever is announcing that construction never finished. The operational translation: field headcount growth must be justified against the *portfolio's* extraction performance, not against pipeline. A function with a strong Extraction Rate has earned the right to grow, because its growth compounds into product. A function whose Extraction Rate is falling has not earned growth even if — especially if — sales wants it, because every added engineer is another vote for the services trajectory.

The era's cautionary structures make the stakes concrete. When a company spins its field function into a separately funded services entity — as the 2025-26 wave of lab spin-offs did, at multi-billion-dollar valuations — the scaffolding has been declared a building. It may be a fine building. It is no longer forward deployed engineering, because the loop back to the product is now an inter-company contract, and Otter's rule applies at entity scale: billable to the project, working for the project.

## Year three, measured

If the model is working, the function's own four metrics should show a signature by the third year — visible in trend even where single quarters wobble.

**Time to First Proof falls** as playbooks compress Landing and the platform's coverage widens; what took a fortnight at year one takes days in the verticals you know.

**Engagements per engineer per year rises** — shorter boxes, smaller teams, agent leverage compounding with platform growth. The same payroll turns the flywheel more times.

**The extraction mix shifts from features to gaps.** Early Ledgers demand whole capabilities (an ingestion tool, an eval harness); mature Ledgers demand edges and configurations of capabilities that exist. This is the platform announcing its own adequacy, one closed gap at a time.

**The portfolio's center of gravity moves outward** — the platform absorbs yesterday's verticals, and the field stands mostly at the newest, least-understood edge: the next industry, the next system class, the next place the product meets a reality it has not seen. Palantir's trajectory again, generalized: the field army did not disband when Foundry shipped; it moved to the next frontier, and the cycle began again.

That last observation resolves the paradox honestly. "The function graduates" does not mean the function disappears — it means each *territory* graduates. Field engineering is permanent as a capability and temporary in every particular application, the way a construction firm is permanent while every scaffold it erects comes down. What must never become permanent is the scaffolding around any one building: any single account (the Permanent Resident, at customer scale), any single vertical (the playbook that replaced looking), any single product gap (the one-third rule, ignored for eight quarters).

A leader wanting the single test of whether their field function is scaling or merely growing can use the graduation question at function scale, the same way the Charter uses it at engagement scale: *name the things the field no longer has to do.* A scaling function answers with a list — ingestion, standard evals, the insurer playbook, all absorbed into product — and the list grows every year. A growing function answers with headcount. The first is Foundry's arc. The second is a consultancy, discovering itself.
