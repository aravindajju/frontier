# Braindump sessions — question set

The normative core must describe Aravind's actual practice, not just the literature. These questions drive the working sessions. Answers get captured in `notes/braindump-answers/` (one file per session) and become the raw material for the Guide and the book's war stories.

Per STYLE.md: push every answer toward a specific engagement — dates, durations, team sizes, what the customer said, what broke. The stories are the book.

## Session 1 — Your practice, concretely

1. Describe your last three engagements: customer type, team shape, duration, what you shipped, how it ended.
2. In a typical engagement week, where does your time actually go? (Coding on customer systems / integration / meetings / politics / travel.)
3. What do you do in the first 48 hours on site? What do you look for first?
4. Tell the story of your best engagement. What made it work?
5. Tell the story of your worst. Where exactly did it go wrong, and when did you know?

## Session 2 — The bright lines (definition)

6. What makes your work not consulting? Answer with an example, not a slogan.
7. Have you seen "FDE" applied to work that was really solutions architecture? What was missing?
8. What are your three criteria for "this is real FDE work"? Test them against edge cases: a pre-sales prototype, a staff-aug contract, a support escalation.
9. Who owns the outcome in your engagements — contractually and in practice? What does owning it actually require of you?

## Session 3 — The loop (productization)

10. Give a concrete case where something you built for one customer became product. How long did it take? Who did the generalizing?
11. Give a case where it should have but didn't. What blocked it?
12. How does field learning reach the product team today — what artifact, what meeting, what cadence? What would you formalize?
13. How do you decide what to overfit deliberately versus build generally from the start?

## Session 4 — Roles and the team

14. Who works alongside you in the field? Is there an Echo-equivalent (domain/politics/adoption owner), and if not, who absorbs that work?
15. What does the customer-side sponsor do when the engagement works? What do they fail to do when it doesn't?
16. Who is your counterpart in core product engineering? How is that relationship structured?
17. What did you have to learn that no engineering job taught you? (Status dynamics, procurement, org politics.)

## Session 5 — Lifecycle, cadence, exit

18. Walk one engagement through its phases as you actually experienced them. What are the natural phase boundaries?
19. What are your graduation criteria — how do you know an engagement is done? Have you ever set an exit date at the start?
20. What recurring events keep an engagement healthy? Which meetings would you delete?
21. Have you seen an engagement that never ended? What kept it alive?

## Session 6 — Rules and metrics

22. If you could enforce three falsifiable rules on any FDE team (like SRE's 50% toil cap), what would they be?
23. What would you measure to know an FDE function is healthy? What did you actually measure, and what did the numbers make you do differently?
24. How fast should first production value land? What number would you defend?
25. What share of deployments needing heavy FDE effort signals a product problem rather than a deployment problem? (Test Wu's 30-40% claim against your experience.)

## Session 7 — Anti-patterns and tailoring

26. Name the failure modes you have personally watched: the engineer failure modes and the org failure modes. One story each.
27. What does burnout look like in this job, and what actually prevented it?
28. Which parts of your practice are universal, and which are specific to your domain, deal size, or company stage?
29. When would you tell a company not to build an FDE function at all?
